## Chapter A1 — Numbers and the Number Line

### A. Word Bank

| Word | Pronunciation | Plain English Meaning | Example |
|------|---------------|----------------------|---------|
| **Natural number** | "NACH-uh-rul" | Counting numbers starting from 1 | 1, 2, 3, ... |
| **Whole number** | — | Naturals plus 0 | 0, 1, 2, 3, ... |
| **Integer** | "IN-tih-jer" | Whole numbers and their negatives | ..., −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, ... |
| **Rational number** | "RASH-un-ul" | Any number that is a fraction of two integers | $\tfrac{3}{4}$, $-\tfrac{7}{2}$, 5 |
| **Irrational number** | "ih-RASH-un-ul" | Cannot be written as a fraction | $\pi$, $\sqrt{2}$ |
| **Real number** | — | All rationals and irrationals together | All numbers on the number line |
| **Number line** | — | A horizontal line showing numbers in order | Visual aid |

### B. Concept Introduction

Every measurement in engineering is a real number on a number line. To the right is positive (more); to the left is negative (less or opposite direction). Zero is the reference. Two ideas matter most:

1. **Order**: any two numbers can be compared (one is less, equal, or greater).
2. **Sign**: positive vs negative captures direction (up/down, tension/compression, left/right).

### C. The Math — Worked Examples

**Place numbers on the number line.** Mark $-3, -1.5, 0, 0.5, 2$.

```
<--- -4 -3 -2 -1  0  1  2  3 -->
       *      *  *  *  *
      -3   -1.5 0 0.5  2
```

**Compare:** $-5$ vs $-2$ → $-5 < -2$ (further left is smaller).

**Absolute value** $|x|$ = distance from 0, always non-negative:
- $|7| = 7$
- $|-7| = 7$
- $|0| = 0$

### D. Later Engineering Use

For now, signs mean direction on a number line:
- $+3$ can mean 3 m above a reference level.
- $-3$ can mean 3 m below that same reference level.
- $|{-3}| = 3$ means the distance from the reference is 3 m.

Later, the same sign idea will describe force direction, compression, tension, and movement. Those engineering words are previews only; the arithmetic skill here is reading sign and size separately.

### E. Common Mistakes

1. **Forgetting that $-5 < -2$.** Bigger absolute value with a minus sign means smaller.
2. **Mixing sign with magnitude.** $-50$ kN is *not* "less force"; it's the same force in the opposite direction.

### F. Chapter Practice Task

> **Tasks:**
> 1. Order from smallest to largest: $-2.5,\ 3,\ 0,\ -7,\ \tfrac{1}{2}$
> 2. Compute $|-12|$ and $|+12|$
> 3. Identify which are integers and which are rational but not integer: $-3,\ \tfrac{2}{5},\ 0,\ 7.5,\ -\tfrac{8}{2}$
>
> **Answers:**
> 1. $-7,\ -2.5,\ 0,\ \tfrac{1}{2},\ 3$
> 2. Both equal 12
> 3. Integers: $-3,\ 0,\ -\tfrac{8}{2}=-4$. Rational not integer: $\tfrac{2}{5},\ 7.5$.

### G. Summary Table

| Concept | Key Fact |
|---------|----------|
| Sign | + right of 0, − left of 0 |
| Order | Further right = larger |
| Absolute value | $|x| \ge 0$ always |

## Chapter A2 — Addition and Subtraction

### A. Word Bank

| Word | Pronunciation | Plain English Meaning |
|------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Sum** | "SUM" | Result of addition |
| **Difference** | "DIF-rens" | Result of subtraction |
| **Addend** | "AD-end" | A number being added |
| **Commutative** | "kuh-MYOO-tuh-tiv" | Order doesn't matter: $a+b=b+a$ |
| **Associative** | "uh-SOH-shee-uh-tiv" | Grouping doesn't matter: $(a+b)+c = a+(b+c)$ |

### B. Concept Introduction

Addition combines quantities. Subtraction takes one quantity away from another. Crucially, **subtracting a number is the same as adding its negative**: $a - b = a + (-b)$. Once you internalize this, all the rules of signs simplify to one rule for addition.

### C. The Math — Worked Examples

**Sign rules for addition:**
- Same signs → add magnitudes, keep the sign: $-4 + (-7) = -11$
- Different signs → subtract magnitudes, keep sign of larger: $-9 + 4 = -5$

**Subtraction:**
- $7 - 12 = -5$
- $-3 - 5 = -3 + (-5) = -8$
- $-10 - (-4) = -10 + 4 = -6$

**Multi-term:** $5 - 3 + 8 - 2 = 8$. Work left to right, treating each term with its own sign.

**Measurement example.** A floor level changes by $+30, -10, +5, -8$ mm during a survey. The total change is $30 - 10 + 5 - 8 = 17\ \mathrm{mm}$.

### D. Later Engineering Use

Structural engineering uses signed addition constantly. Later, upward and downward forces will be added the same way survey changes were added above. For this chapter, the goal is only to become comfortable combining positive and negative numbers.

### E. Common Mistakes

1. **Sign flip on subtracting a negative.** $7 - (-3) = 10$, not 4.
2. **Lining up decimals wrong.** $12.5 + 3.07$ → align decimal points: $15.57$, not $15.12$.

### F. Chapter Practice Task

> Compute:
> 1. $-6 + 11$
> 2. $4 - 9$
> 3. $-3 - (-8)$
> 4. $12.4 - 5.07 + 0.6$
> 5. Sum of signed changes: $+25, -40, +20, -8$
>
> **Answers:** 1) 5  2) −5  3) 5  4) 7.93  5) −3

### G. Summary Table

| Operation | Rule |
|-----------|------|
| Same signs add | Add magnitudes, keep sign |
| Different signs add | Subtract magnitudes, sign of larger |
| Subtract | Add the negative |

## Chapter A3 — Multiplication and Division

### A. Word Bank

| Word | Pronunciation | Plain English Meaning |
|------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Product** | "PROD-ukt" | Result of multiplication |
| **Quotient** | "KWOH-shent" | Result of division |
| **Factor** | "FAK-tor" | A number being multiplied |
| **Divisor** | "dih-VYE-zor" | The number you divide by |
| **Inverse operation** | "IN-vurs op-er-AY-shun" | An operation that undoes another operation |

### B. Concept Introduction

Multiplication is repeated addition: $4 \times 3 = 4 + 4 + 4 = 12$. Division is the inverse: $12 \div 3 = 4$ (how many 3's make 12).

For signs, the rule is simple and symmetric:
- $(+)(+) = +$
- $(-)(-) = +$
- $(+)(-) = -$
- $(-)(+) = -$

Same idea for division.

### C. The Math — Worked Examples

**Sign examples:**
- $(-3)(-4) = 12$
- $(-7)(2) = -14$
- $(-15) \div 3 = -5$
- $(-20) \div (-4) = 5$

**Division:** use equal groups and inverse multiplication.
$24 \div 6 = 4$ because $6 \times 4 = 24$.

**Measurement example.** A drawing has 4 equal bays, each 6 m long. Total length = $4 \times 6 = 24$ m.

### D. Later Engineering Use

Multiplication and division will later convert between totals and rates: total length from repeated bay spacing, total cost from cost per meter, and eventually total load from load per meter. The engineering words are not needed yet; the skill is recognizing "per" as division and repeated equal groups as multiplication.

### E. Common Mistakes

1. **Forgetting the sign rule of two negatives.** $(-2)(-5) = +10$, not $-10$.
2. **Dividing by zero.** Undefined. Always check the denominator.
3. **Thinking division always makes a number smaller.** $24 \div 6$ is smaller than 24, but $6 \div 6 = 1$ and $0 \div 6 = 0$. Division asks about equal groups; it does not follow one size rule.

### F. Chapter Practice Task

> Compute:
> 1. $(-6)(7)$
> 2. $(-4)(-9)$
> 3. $-48 \div -6$
> 4. $56 \div 7$
> 5. A 200 m total length is split equally among 5 identical segments. Find the length of each segment.
>
> **Answers:** 1) −42  2) 36  3) 8  4) 8  5) 40 m

### G. Summary Table

| Operation | Sign Rule |
|-----------|-----------|
| Two same | Result + |
| Two different | Result − |
| Divide by 0 | Undefined |

## Chapter A4 — Fractions

### A. Word Bank

| Word | Pronunciation | Plain English Meaning |
|------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Fraction** | "FRAK-shun" | One whole divided into parts: $\tfrac{a}{b}$ |
| **Numerator** | "NOO-mer-ay-tor" | Top of the fraction |
| **Denominator** | "dee-NOM-ih-nay-tor" | Bottom of the fraction |
| **Equivalent fraction** | "ee-KWIV-uh-lent" | Same value, different look: $\tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{2}{4}$ |
| **Lowest terms** | — | Numerator and denominator share no common factor |
| **Common denominator** | — | Same bottom number, needed for adding |

### B. Concept Introduction

A fraction $\tfrac{a}{b}$ means "$a$ parts of $b$ equal pieces." Multiplying or dividing both top and bottom by the same number gives an equivalent fraction. Reducing to lowest terms makes results readable and prevents arithmetic errors later.

### C. The Math — Worked Examples

**Addition / subtraction:** need a common denominator.
$\dfrac{1}{3} + \dfrac{1}{4} = \dfrac{4}{12} + \dfrac{3}{12} = \dfrac{7}{12}$

**Multiplication:** multiply tops, multiply bottoms.
$\dfrac{2}{3} \times \dfrac{4}{5} = \dfrac{8}{15}$

**Division:** multiply by the reciprocal.
$\dfrac{2}{3} \div \dfrac{4}{5} = \dfrac{2}{3} \times \dfrac{5}{4} = \dfrac{10}{12} = \dfrac{5}{6}$

**Reducing:** $\dfrac{18}{24} = \dfrac{18 \div 6}{24 \div 6} = \dfrac{3}{4}$

**Mixed to improper:** $2\tfrac{1}{3} = \dfrac{2 \cdot 3 + 1}{3} = \dfrac{7}{3}$.

**Engineering example.** A beam spans 6 m. Mid-span is $\tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 6 = 3$ m. Quarter-span is $\tfrac{1}{4} \cdot 6 = 1.5$ m.

### D. Later Engineering Use

Fractions will later appear in formulas because engineering often deals with halves, thirds, quarters, and ratios. For now, use fractions in familiar geometry and measurement ideas:
- Half of a 6 m span is $\tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 6 = 3$ m.
- Quarter of a 6 m span is $\tfrac{1}{4} \cdot 6 = 1.5$ m.
- A factor of $\tfrac{9}{10}$ means keeping 9 parts out of 10.

### E. Common Mistakes

1. **Adding without common denominator.** $\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{3} \neq \tfrac{2}{5}$. Correct: $\tfrac{3}{6} + \tfrac{2}{6} = \tfrac{5}{6}$.
2. **Inverting the wrong fraction in division.** Flip the *divisor*, not the dividend.
3. **Forgetting to reduce.** $\tfrac{8}{12}$ should be $\tfrac{2}{3}$.

### F. Chapter Practice Task

> Compute and reduce:
> 1. $\tfrac{2}{5} + \tfrac{1}{4}$
> 2. $\tfrac{3}{4} - \tfrac{1}{6}$
> 3. $\tfrac{2}{3} \times \tfrac{9}{8}$
> 4. $\tfrac{5}{6} \div \tfrac{10}{3}$
> 5. Find one eighth of $12 \times 4^2$.
>
> **Answers:** 1) $\tfrac{13}{20}$  2) $\tfrac{7}{12}$  3) $\tfrac{3}{4}$  4) $\tfrac{1}{4}$  5) $24$

### G. Summary Table

| Operation | Method |
|-----------|--------|
| Add/Subtract | Common denominator |
| Multiply | Tops × tops, bottoms × bottoms |
| Divide | Multiply by reciprocal |
| Simplify | Divide both by GCD |

## Chapter A5 — Decimals and Percentages

### A. Word Bank

| Word | Pronunciation | Plain English Meaning |
|------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Decimal** | "DES-ih-mul" | Number with a fractional part separated by a point |
| **Percent** | "per-SENT" | "Per hundred" — fraction with denominator 100 |
| **Percentage** | "per-SENT-ij" | A part expressed as percent |

### B. Concept Introduction

Decimals are fractions with denominators of 10, 100, 1000, ... A percent is just a decimal expressing parts per hundred:
$25\% = \dfrac{25}{100} = 0.25$

Engineering uses both: load factors are sometimes 1.4 (decimal) or 140% (same thing).

### C. The Math — Worked Examples

**Decimal ↔ percent:**
- $0.05 = 5\%$
- $1.25 = 125\%$
- $0.4 = 40\%$

**Percent of a number:** multiply by the decimal form.
- $20\%$ of $300 = 0.20 \times 300 = 60$
- $115\%$ of $80 = 1.15 \times 80 = 92$

**Increase / decrease:**
- Increase 50 by 10%: $50 \times 1.10 = 55$
- Decrease 50 by 10%: $50 \times 0.90 = 45$

**Measurement example.** A 5 m length is reduced by 20%. New length $= 0.80 \times 5 = 4$ m.

### D. Engineering Use

- Design often compares values as percentages: 80% used, 20% remaining, 5% increase, 15% reduction.
- Later, load factors and design ratios will use the same decimal-percent conversions.
- Slope can be written as a percent: 1% slope means 1 cm rise per 1 m run.

### E. Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing 0.5% with 0.5 (50%).** $0.5\% = 0.005$, not $0.5$.
2. **Adding percentages of different bases.** "20% off, then 10% off" ≠ "30% off."
3. **Misplacing decimal in design.** Forgetting that 0.04 (4%) reinforcement ratio is large; 0.4 would be impossibly high.

### F. Chapter Practice Task

> 1. Convert: $0.075$ to %; $7\%$ to decimal
> 2. Compute: 12% of 250
> 3. A total of 800 units includes 200 units that may be reduced by 15%. What is the new total?
> 4. A value must increase by 8%. What multiplier should you use?
>
> **Answers:** 1) 7.5%, 0.07  2) 30  3) Reduced LL = $0.85 \cdot 200 = 170$; total = $600 + 170 = 770$ kN  4) Multiply capacity by at least $1.08$.

### G. Summary Table

| Concept | Conversion |
|---------|-----------|
| % → decimal | Divide by 100 |
| Decimal → % | Multiply by 100 |
| % of $N$ | Decimal × $N$ |
| Increase $p\%$ | Multiply by $(1 + p/100)$ |
| Decrease $p\%$ | Multiply by $(1 - p/100)$ |

## Chapter A6 — Powers, Roots, and Scientific Notation

### A. Word Bank

| Word | Pronunciation | Plain English Meaning |
|------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Power** | "POW-er" | Repeated multiplication: $a^n$ |
| **Base** | "BAYS" | The number being raised |
| **Exponent** | "EX-poh-nent" | How many times to multiply |
| **Square** | — | Power of 2: $a^2$ |
| **Cube** | — | Power of 3: $a^3$ |
| **Square root** | — | Reverse of squaring: $\sqrt{a}$ |
| **Cube root** | — | Reverse of cubing: $\sqrt[3]{a}$ |
| **Scientific notation** | — | $a \times 10^n$ for compact large/small numbers |

### B. Concept Introduction

A power $a^n$ means "$a$ multiplied by itself $n$ times":
$3^4 = 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 3 = 81$

A root undoes a power. $\sqrt{49} = 7$ because $7^2 = 49$.

Scientific notation handles the huge range of structural numbers — concrete strength is 30 million Pa = $3 \times 10^7$ Pa = 30 MPa. Steel modulus is $200{,}000{,}000{,}000$ Pa = $2 \times 10^{11}$ Pa = 200 GPa.

### C. The Math — Worked Examples

**Power rules:**
- $a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}$ (e.g., $2^3 \cdot 2^4 = 2^7 = 128$)
- $a^m / a^n = a^{m-n}$
- $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$
- $a^0 = 1$ (for $a \neq 0$)
- $a^{-n} = 1/a^n$

**Common squares to memorize:** $1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100, 121, 144$.

**Scientific notation:** move decimal until one nonzero digit is left of it.
- $5{,}600{,}000 = 5.6 \times 10^6$
- $0.000034 = 3.4 \times 10^{-5}$

**Multiplying in scientific notation:**
$(2 \times 10^4)(3 \times 10^5) = 6 \times 10^9$

**Engineering example.** Steel modulus $E = 200$ GPa = $200 \times 10^9$ Pa = $2 \times 10^{11}$ Pa.

### D. Later Engineering Use

Powers and roots are the arithmetic behind square areas, cubic volumes, and scientific notation:
- A square footing with side $s$ has area $s^2$.
- A cube with side $s$ has volume $s^3$.
- Very large or small quantities are easier to read in scientific notation.

Later chapters will use the same exponent rules in beam stiffness and deflection formulas.

### E. Common Mistakes

1. **$(-3)^2 = 9$ vs $-3^2 = -9$.** Without parentheses, the exponent applies before the negative sign.
2. **Adding exponents incorrectly.** $a^m + a^n \neq a^{m+n}$. Adding only works for *multiplication* of same-base powers.
3. **Mixing up $10^9$ (giga) and $10^6$ (mega).** A factor of 1000 difference.

### F. Chapter Practice Task

> 1. Compute: $5^3,\ (-2)^4,\ \sqrt{144},\ \sqrt[3]{27}$
> 2. Simplify: $x^5 \cdot x^3$; $(y^2)^4$; $z^7 / z^4$
> 3. Convert: $4{,}500{,}000$ to scientific; $7.2 \times 10^{-4}$ to decimal
> 4. A rectangular box is $0.3$ m wide, $0.6$ m tall, and $0.6$ m long. Compute its volume.
>
> **Answers:** 1) 125, 16, 12, 3  2) $x^8, y^8, z^3$  3) $4.5 \times 10^6$, $0.00072$  4) $0.3 \times 0.6 \times 0.6 = 0.108\ \mathrm{m^3}$

### G. Summary Table

| Rule | Example |
|------|---------|
| $a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}$ | $2^3 \cdot 2^4 = 128$ |
| $a^m / a^n = a^{m-n}$ | $10^7 / 10^3 = 10^4$ |
| $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$ | $(3^2)^3 = 729$ |
| $\sqrt{a^2} = |a|$ | $\sqrt{25} = 5$ |
| Sci. notation | $a \times 10^n$, $1 \le a < 10$ |

## Chapter A7 — Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

### A. Word Bank

| Word | Pronunciation | Plain English Meaning |
|------|---------------|----------------------|
| **PEMDAS / BODMAS** | — | Acronym for the order of operations |
| **Parenthesis** | "puh-REN-thuh-sis" | Grouping symbol $(\,\cdot\,)$ |
| **Operation** | "op-er-AY-shun" | An arithmetic action: + − × ÷ ^ |
| **Precedence** | "PRES-eh-dens" | Which operation goes first |

### B. Concept Introduction

When an expression mixes operations, evaluate them in this order:
1. **P** — Parentheses (innermost first)
2. **E** — Exponents (powers, roots)
3. **MD** — Multiplication and Division (left to right, same level)
4. **AS** — Addition and Subtraction (left to right, same level)

Without this rule, $2 + 3 \times 4$ could equal either 20 or 14. PEMDAS makes it 14.

### C. The Math — Worked Examples

**$2 + 3 \times 4$**
$= 2 + 12 = 14$ ✓ (not 20)

**$(2 + 3) \times 4$**
$= 5 \times 4 = 20$

**$3^2 + 4 \cdot 5$**
$= 9 + 20 = 29$

**$\dfrac{12 - 4 \cdot 2}{2}$**
Top: $12 - 8 = 4$. Bottom: 2. Result: 2.

**$10 - 6 \div 2 + 4$**
$= 10 - 3 + 4 = 11$ (left-to-right for ÷ and + −)

**Formula example.** Compute $\dfrac{5L^2}{8}$ for $L = 6$:
$\dfrac{5 \cdot 6^2}{8} = \dfrac{5 \cdot 36}{8} = \dfrac{180}{8} = 22.5$

(Power before multiplication.)

### D. Engineering Use

Every formula later in this book relies on PEMDAS. A single slip in operation order can give answers that are far too large or far too small.

### E. Common Mistakes

1. **Adding before multiplying.** $4 + 3 \cdot 2 = 10$, not $14$ ... wait, $14$. Correct! $3\cdot2 = 6$, plus $4 = 10$. Beware: $4 + 3 \cdot 2$: many people compute $4+3 = 7$, then $\cdot 2 = 14$. Wrong.
2. **Treating ÷ as lower than ×.** Same precedence — go left to right.
3. **Forgetting brackets in formulas typed in software.** $w \cdot L \wedge 2 / 8$ in some calculators is parsed differently than expected. Use parentheses generously: $(w \cdot L^2) / 8$.

### F. Chapter Practice Task

> Compute:
> 1. $7 + 2 \cdot 3$
> 2. $(7 + 2) \cdot 3$
> 3. $4^2 - 6 \div 3$
> 4. $\dfrac{8 + 2 \cdot 5}{3}$
> 5. Evaluate $\dfrac{10 \cdot 2^3}{3 \cdot 200 \cdot 0.0001}$
>
> **Answers:** 1) 13  2) 27  3) 14  4) 6  5) $\dfrac{10 \cdot 8}{3 \cdot 200 \cdot 0.0001} = \dfrac{80}{0.06} = 1333.\overline{3}$

### G. Summary Table

| Step | Operation |
|------|-----------|
| 1 | Parentheses |
| 2 | Exponents/Roots |
| 3 | Multiplication & Division (L→R) |
| 4 | Addition & Subtraction (L→R) |

## Chapter A8 — Estimation, Rounding, and Significant Figures

### A. Word Bank

| Word | Pronunciation | Plain English Meaning |
|------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Estimate** | "ES-tih-mayt" | Approximate value, quick judgment |
| **Round** | — | Cut off digits to a chosen place |
| **Significant figure** | — | Digits that carry actual measurement information |
| **Order of magnitude** | — | Power-of-ten size |

### B. Concept Introduction

Engineering numbers are *measurements*, not exact values. Reporting a beam length as $6.000000\ \mathrm{m}$ is misleading — the tape measure is good to maybe a millimeter.

**Significant figures** capture the precision of a measurement. Final answers should not have more sig-figs than the inputs.

**Estimation** lets you sanity-check ETABS output. If you expect "around 100 kN·m" and the software reports 1500 kN·m, something is wrong.

### C. The Math — Worked Examples

**Rounding rules:** look at the digit after the cutoff place.
- 0–4 → round down (keep)
- 5–9 → round up

$3.146 \to 3.15$ (3 dp); $3.1416 \to 3.14$ (3 sf).

**Significant figure counting:**
- $200$ — ambiguous (1, 2, or 3 sf depending on context); $2.00 \times 10^2$ unambiguous (3 sf)
- $0.0035$ — 2 sf (leading zeros don't count)
- $1.020$ — 4 sf (trailing zero after decimal counts)

**Multiplication / division:** result has the same sf count as the *least* precise input.
$3.21 \times 4.5 = 14.445 \to 14$ (2 sf, limited by 4.5).

**Estimation example (back-of-envelope).** Total dead load on a 5-story building, 20 m × 15 m floor:
- Slab DL ≈ 4.5 kPa × 300 m² × 5 stories ≈ 6750 kN
- Round to 7000 kN ≈ "$7 \times 10^3$ kN"

Use this to check ETABS's reported 6800 kN — within 5%, ✓.

### D. Engineering Use

- Pre-design sizing: estimate before sketching.
- Sanity-check software output.
- Report results to a sensible precision (a moment of $124.683\ \mathrm{kN \cdot m}$ implies 6-figure precision that the inputs almost certainly don't support).
- ETABS reports many digits; *use* only the meaningful ones in design.

### E. Common Mistakes

1. **Reporting 8 digits from ETABS.** "Max moment = 124.6748293 kN·m" pretends to a precision the inputs don't have.
2. **Rounding too early.** Keep 1–2 extra digits in intermediate steps; round only at the end.
3. **Skipping the estimation step.** A factor-of-10 error in input is invisible without one.

### F. Chapter Practice Task

> 1. Round $7.4836$ to 2 dp; to 3 sf
> 2. Count sig figs: $0.00420,\ 5.060,\ 200,\ 1.0 \times 10^4$
> 3. Estimate $w L^2 / 8$ for $w \approx 6\ \mathrm{kN/m},\ L \approx 5\ \mathrm{m}$
> 4. (Engineering sanity check) ETABS reports column axial $= 482.317$ kN. Your hand estimate from tributary area was $\approx 480$ kN. Comment.
>
> **Answers:** 1) 7.48; 7.48  2) 3, 4, 1–3 (ambiguous), 2  3) $6 \cdot 25 / 8 \approx 19\ \mathrm{kN \cdot m}$  4) Match within 0.5% — accept the ETABS value, report as 482 kN (3 sf).

### G. Summary Table

| Concept | Rule |
|---------|------|
| Round to N dp | Look at digit (N+1); 0–4 down, 5–9 up |
| Sig figs (×, ÷) | Use least precise input |
| Sig figs (+, −) | Use least precise *decimal place* |
| Estimate first | 1 sig fig is enough for sanity |
